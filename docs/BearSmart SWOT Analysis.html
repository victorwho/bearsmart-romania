  • S2

W
Gaps today

Weaknesses

  • W1
    Dense, demo-flavoured home screen.Index.html stacks 9+ panels (Spotlight, Localities, Contacts, Plan, Report, Alerts, Published Reports, Admin, Ops) in one column — everything is one tap away, nothing is one decision away.
  • W2
    Role switcher in the masthead.Exposing Public / Resident / Town Admin / Moderator / Reviewer to every visitor reads as a demo, not a product. Confuses trust signals.
  • W3
    Localities feel like a spreadsheet.The directory is a filterable grid — no emotional draw, no “visit this place,” no imagery, no pride-of-place narrative.
  • W4
    No first-run orientation.A tourist landing cold is shown a map, a role dropdown, and a sheet of tabs. No “What should I do this weekend?” (User Story #36) entry point.
  • W5
    Emergency path unclear."Report" and "Emergency 112" are button-siblings in the header. PRD mandates clear separation; today they compete.
O
Openings

Opportunities

  • O1
    Become the Romania travel lens for mountain country.No product today sits between Google Maps and a tourism board with real-time safety + town certification. Own that gap.
  • O2
    Certification as civic prestige.Public scorecards, renewal badges, and town-vs-town progress turn compliance into reputation currency for mayors.
  • O3
    Tourist subscriptions as a growth loop.Trip-scoped, auto-expiring alert subscriptions (PRD US #7) are lightweight acquisition for seasonal visitors — the most common first-touch audience.
  • O4
    EU funding & ESG narrative.Human–wildlife coexistence is a fundable cross-border story. A pilot with published metrics is the artefact grant reviewers want.
  • O5
    Partnerships with beekeepers, apiaries, guesthouses.Bear-safe business badge is an under-exploited B2B flywheel — each badge is both a compliance win and a marketing asset the business distributes for free.
T
Risks

Threats

  • T1
    Misinformation via unmoderated reports.A single viral false-location sighting could undermine trust or create crowds. Moderation latency is the single biggest reputational risk.
  • T2
    Low town-admin engagement.If pilot mayors don't post alerts weekly, town pages go stale and the retention loop dies. Admin onboarding is an existential concern, not a polish item.
  • T3
    Official agency territorialism.National wildlife and emergency services may see the platform as duplicative. Mitigation: read-only, source-of-record framing; never claim dispatch role.
  • T4
    Seasonality collapse.Bear activity is seasonal; usage may trough in winter. Without preparedness + off-season education loops the app feels dead 5 months a year.
  • T5
    Privacy exposure of rural residents.Precise sighting pins near isolated homes could identify households. Geo-fuzzing rules must ship before public launch, not after an incident.

03 / Platform Appeal

Three pillars for mass appeal
without losing depth

The platform thesis: BearSmart wins when a tourist, a mayor, and a beekeeper all want to open it weekly — for different reasons but the same trust.

Pillar 01

Become the Carpathian
trust layer

Position the platform not as a bear site, but as the definitive real-time civic safety layer for Romanian mountain country — bears today, floods and road hazards tomorrow.

  • Single-source claim. Alerts authored by certified town halls carry an official mark and sync to Google, search snippets, hotel concierges.
  • Emergency parity. Clear, designed-in separation between informational reporting and 112 escalation on every surface.
  • Verified, graded truth. Every public fact tagged unverified / community-confirmed / officially verified.
Pillar 02

Make every locality
feel like a destination

Treat each certified town as a brand we co-promote. The BearSmart badge should be something a guesthouse wants on its front window, and a mayor wants on the town-hall website.

  • Town-as-product page. Hero photography, a one-line pitch, seasonal mood, resident voice, business badges, live alert strip.
  • Tourist trip mode. "I'm visiting next weekend" generates a temporary subscription, a prep plan, and an offline-ready pocket guide.
  • Public scorecards. Civic pride metrics — households bear-proofed, businesses certified, incident trend — published per town, updated quarterly.
Pillar 03

Design for return,
not for download

The current MVP has excellent first-visit coverage but weak return-day-7 hooks. The next six months are about building reasons to reopen.

  • Seasonal loops. Spring emergence, berry season, autumn hyperphagia — auto-scheduled national campaigns with per-town copy.
  • Preparedness streaks. Checklists that persist across seasons; a household profile that graduates from "new" → "prepared" → "mentor."
  • Neighbour verification. Reports earn a "3 neighbours confirm" badge — social proof turns a one-way form into a local conversation.

The Core Loop

Alert → Act → See progress → Come back

The PRD already identifies this as the central product motion. The redesign makes each step a single-tap surface with clear feedback — today most steps still require 2–3 taps and the progress feedback is missing entirely.

Instrumentation rule: every step in this loop emits a named event. If we can't answer "did 100 residents who opened an alert complete one checklist item this week," the loop is not shipped.

Promotion lever 01

Embeddable town widget

A small, branded "BearSmart Status" card a town can paste into its own tourism site. One line of embed code. Live alert + certification state. Drives backlinks and visibility.

Promotion lever 02

Seasonal press kit

Quarterly auto-generated "State of BearSmart" PDFs per locality — photography, stats, mayor quotes — ready for local press and newsletters without a comms team.

Promotion lever 03

Bear-safe business directory

Every guesthouse, restaurant, apiary that earns a badge is listed on the town page and gets a printable window sticker. Each badge is a free distribution surface.
